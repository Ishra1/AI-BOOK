# Bipedal Locomotion

## Learning Outcomes
By the end of this module, you will be able to:
- Understand the definition, advantages, and inherent challenges of bipedal locomotion in robotics.
- Grasp the fundamental principles of robot modeling, kinematics, and dynamics as applied to bipedal systems.
- Comprehend key stability concepts like ZMP and LIPM, and various stability control strategies.
- Explore methods for gait generation and trajectory planning for bipedal robots.
- Identify the essential hardware components, sensors, and simulation environments used in bipedal robotics.
- Gain insight into advanced topics, future directions, and practical implementation considerations for bipedal locomotion.

---

<h2>1. Introduction to Bipedal Locomotion</h2>

<h3>1.1 What is Bipedal Locomotion?</h3>
Content for: Definition and examples (humans, robots), Advantages (maneuverability, navigating complex terrain), Challenges (stability, energy efficiency, control complexity)

<h3>1.2 Why Study Bipedal Locomotion?</h3>
Content for: Applications (humanoid robots, prosthetics, exoskeletons), Scientific interest (understanding human movement)

<h3>1.3 Key Concepts & Terminology</h3>
Content for: Degrees of Freedom (DoF), Center of Mass (CoM), Zero Moment Point (ZMP), Support Polygon/Region, Gait cycles (stance phase, swing phase, double support)

<h2>2. Robot Modeling & Kinematics</h2>

<h3>2.1 Rigid Body Mechanics Review</h3>
Content for: Coordinate frames, transformations (rotation matrices, homogeneous transforms), Denavit-Hartenberg (DH) parameters (brief overview)

<h3>2.2 Forward Kinematics</h3>
Content for: Calculating end-effector positions from joint angles, Application to robot legs and torso

<h3>2.3 Inverse Kinematics</h3>
Content for: Calculating joint angles for desired end-effector positions, Analytical vs. numerical solutions (Jacobian-based methods)

<h3>2.4 Robot Structure & Configuration</h3>
Content for: Typical DoF for bipedal robots (e.g., 6 DoF per leg + torso/arms), Common joint types (revolute, prismatic)

<h2>3. Robot Dynamics</h2>

<h3>3.1 Lagrangian Dynamics / Newton-Euler Formulation (overview)</h3>
Content for: Equations of motion for multi-link systems, Mass matrix, Coriolis/centrifugal terms, gravity terms

<h3>3.2 Center of Mass (CoM) Dynamics</h3>
Content for: Calculating CoM position and velocity, Importance of CoM for stability

<h3>3.3 Zero Moment Point (ZMP) Concept</h3>
Content for: Definition and physical interpretation, Relationship to stability and ground reaction forces, ZMP equations for bipedal robots

<h3>3.4 Linear Inverted Pendulum Model (LIPM)</h3>
Content for: Simplified model for walking dynamics, Equations and properties (exponential behavior), Predictive control using LIPM

<h2>4. Stability Control Strategies</h2>

<h3>4.1 Static Stability vs. Dynamic Stability</h3>
Content for: When is static stability sufficient? (e.g., standing), Why dynamic stability is crucial for walking

<h3>4.2 ZMP-based Control</h3>
Content for: Reference ZMP trajectories, Feedback control to track ZMP (e.g., CoM adjustment), Preview control for ZMP tracking

<h3>4.3 Whole-Body Control (WBC)</h3>
Content for: Prioritized tasks (balance, posture, end-effector control), Optimization-based approaches (quadratic programming)

<h3>4.4 Model Predictive Control (MPC)</h3>
Content for: Predicting future states and optimizing control inputs, Application to bipedal walking (e.g., LIPM-MPC)

<h2>5. Gait Generation & Planning</h2>

<h3>5.1 Trajectory Generation</h3>
Content for: Footstep planning (placement, orientation), CoM trajectory generation (e.g., using LIPM), Swing leg trajectory generation (splines, polynomials)

<h3>5.2 Different Gait Patterns</h3>
Content for: Walking, running, stepping over obstacles, Rhythmic vs. non-rhythmic gaits

<h3>5.3 Reinforcement Learning for Gait Generation (brief)</h3>
Content for: Training policies for robust locomotion, Sim-to-real transfer challenges

<h2>6. Hardware & Sensors</h2>

<h3>6.1 Actuators</h3>
Content for: Types (DC motors, servo motors, hydraulic/pneumatic), Gear ratios, torque, speed considerations

<h3>6.2 Sensors</h3>
Content for: **Proprioceptive:** Encoders (joint angles), IMUs (orientation, angular velocity, acceleration), Force/Torque sensors (ground reaction forces), **Exteroceptive:** Lidar, cameras (for perception, path planning - brief mention)

<h3>6.3 Robot Platforms</h3>
Content for: Examples of bipedal robots (ASIMO, Atlas, Boston Dynamics, open-source platforms), Mechanical design considerations (weight, stiffness, joint limits)

<h2>7. Simulation & Implementation</h2>

<h3>7.1 Robotics Simulation Environments</h3>
Content for: ROS (Robot Operating System), Gazebo, PyBullet, MuJoCo, Webots, Setting up a bipedal robot model in simulation

<h3>7.2 Control Architectures</h3>
Content for: Real-time operating systems (RTOS), Communication protocols

<h3>7.3 Practical Considerations</h3>
Content for: Debugging in simulation vs. hardware, Safety precautions for hardware testing, Sensor calibration
